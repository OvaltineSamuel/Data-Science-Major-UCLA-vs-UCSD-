# Data-Science-Major-UCLA-vs-UCSD-

# UCLA Statistics & Data Science vs. UCSD Data Science

Prepared May 2, 2026, using the current UCLA Statistics & Data Science department worksheet/contract and UC San Diego Data Science catalog/HDSI advising pages.

## Transfer Caveat

Your “I only need upper-division classes” assumption is mostly right for UCLA if all lower-division prep is satisfied, but it is risky for UCSD.

| Issue | UCLA Statistics & Data Science BS | UCSD Data Science BS |
|---|---|---|
| Transfer/pre-major status | Transfer admits are classified as **Pre-Statistics & Data Science** until lower-division prep is completed. UCLA admission notes list intro statistics, calculus through multivariable, and, for Fall 2026 and later applicants, linear algebra. The UCLA major contract also includes Stats 20 and Math 33A as prep. | Data Science is capped/selective. UCSD transfer prep for admission is math plus programming, but the major itself still includes UCSD lower-division DSC foundations: DSC 10, 20, 30, 40A-B, 80, COGS 9, math, and a subject-domain sequence. HDSI says the major currently requires a minimum of 8 quarters for transfer students. |
| Upper-division load | 52 units: 9 required core courses + 4 electives. | 60 units: 8 core courses + 2-course senior project + 20 units of electives. |
| Practical transfer implication | Cleaner two-year upper-division plan if Stats 20 and all prep are complete. | More likely to require lower-division DSC sequencing after transfer unless you have approved equivalents/petitions. This can delay access to upper-division DSC courses. |

## Graduation Requirement Overview

| Category | UCLA Statistics & Data Science BS | UCSD Data Science BS |
|---|---|---|
| Upper-division total | **52 units** | **60 units** |
| Required/core structure | Stats 100A-B-C, 101A-B-C, 102A-B-C | Probability/statistics choice; MATH 189 or DSC 152; DSC 100, 102, 106; ML/AI/data mining choices |
| Senior project | No separate senior capstone listed in the 2025 major contract. | Required: DSC 180A-B, 8 units. |
| Electives | 4 upper-division electives from approved Statistics, Math, Communication, Digital Humanities, Economics, EEB, and Psychology options. | 20 units of upper-division electives from approved DSC/domain courses, plus up to 8 petitioned units in a subject domain. |
| Grade policy | Major contract says courses must be taken for letter grade; prep requires C or better and 2.5 prep GPA; upper-division major GPA must stay 2.0+. | Upper-division major courses must be letter grade unless P/NP-only; minimum C- required. |
| Overall flavor | Statistics-first: probability, mathematical statistics, linear models, regression/design, computational statistics in R. | Data-science systems/project-first: data management, scalable analytics, visualization, ML/AI, domain electives, senior project. |

## UCLA Required Upper-Division Core

| Requirement area | Course | Units | Description |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Probability/theory | Stats 100A: Introduction to Probability | 4 | Probability distributions, random variables/vectors, and expectation. This is the first theory course and feeds the rest of the 100-series. |
| Probability/theory | Stats 100B: Introduction to Mathematical Statistics | 4 | Survey sampling, estimation, hypothesis testing, data summaries, and one- and two-sample problems. |
| Probability/theory | Stats 100C: Linear Models | 4 | Theory of linear models using a matrix approach to regression; model fitting, sums of squares, general linear hypothesis tests, inference, Gauss-Markov ideas, residuals, principal component regression, and stepwise procedures. |
| Applied statistics | Stats 101A: Introduction to Data Analysis and Regression | 4 | Applied regression analysis, including multiple regression and generalized linear models such as logistic regression; diagnostics, graphics, and bootstrap methods for influence. |
| Applied statistics | Stats 101B: Introduction to Design and Analysis of Experiment | 4 | How to collect and analyze experimental data: randomization, blocking, completely randomized designs, ANOVA, multiple comparisons, power, sample size, and block designs. |
| Applied statistics | Stats 101C: Introduction to Statistical Models and Data Mining | 4 | Advanced/applied statistical modeling and data-mining-oriented analysis. The catalog framing emphasizes statistical models and data mining; UCLA course listings commonly connect it to modern regression/modeling workflows. |
| Computational statistics | Stats 102A: Introduction to Computational Statistics with R | 4 | Computational statistics in R: statistical graphics, root finding, simulation, randomization tests, bootstrapping, and intermediate/advanced R programming. |
| Computational statistics | Stats 102B: Introduction to Computation and Optimization for Statistics | 4 | Computational and optimization methods for statisticians: vector/matrix computation, multivariate normal distribution, PCA, clustering, gradient methods, EM for missing data, and dynamic programming. |
| Computational statistics | Stats 102C: Introduction to Monte Carlo Methods | 4 | Markov chain Monte Carlo and scientific computing: random-number generation, rejection/importance sampling, Markov chain convergence, Metropolis and Gibbs sampling, simulated tempering, and implementation. |

## UCLA Elective Requirement

UCLA requires **4 upper-division electives**. These are options, not all required courses.

| Elective bucket | Eligible options from the UCLA major contract | What the bucket lets you emphasize |
|---|---|---|
| Statistics electives | Stats 112-199, including Stats 140XP and 141XP; max 4 units each of Stats 195 and Stats 199 may count. | Most direct fit: advanced statistics, consulting, ML, Bayesian statistics, time series, spatial statistics, finance, research or special studies depending on offering. |
| Math electives | Math 115A, 131A, 131B, 151A, 151B, 156, 170B, 171, 177, 178A, 178B, 178C | More theoretical/mathematical route: proof-based linear algebra, analysis, numerical methods, machine learning/math modeling, actuarial/finance topics. |
| Social/data-domain electives | Comm 153 or 188C; Digital Humanities 101; Econ 143, 144, 147; Ecology and Evolutionary Biology C172; Psych 142H | Applied domain route: communication/data, digital humanities, economics, ecology/evolution, or psychology/statistical applications. |

## UCSD Required Upper-Division Core

UCSD requires **32 units of core**, **8 units of senior project**, and **20 units of electives**.

| Requirement area | Course/options | Units | Description |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Probability/statistics foundation | ECON 120A **or** MATH 183 **or** MATH 181A | 4 | Choose one. ECON 120A covers probability/statistics for economics and spreadsheets; MATH 183 covers probability, common discrete/continuous distributions, CLT, graphical data analysis, confidence intervals, tests, and curve fitting; MATH 181A is the more mathematical-statistics option with multivariate distributions, functions of random variables, estimation, confidence intervals, and hypothesis testing. |
| Exploratory data analysis/inference | MATH 189 **or** DSC 152 | 4 | MATH 189 covers quantitative/statistical methods for data, especially big data; probability review, R-based processing/analysis/visualization, inference, sampling, hypothesis tests, bootstrap, regression, and diagnostics. HDSI also lists DSC 152 as an alternative on its current requirements page. |
| Data management | DSC 100: Introduction to Data Management | 4 | Large-scale data storage and management using relational/SQL systems; SQL model and query language, schema design, query optimization basics, database architecture, and database-backed applications. |
| Scalable systems | DSC 102: Systems for Scalable Analytics | 4 | Computing infrastructure for large-scale analytics: memory hierarchy, distributed systems, model selection, heterogeneous datasets, deployment, MapReduce/Hadoop, Spark, dataflow operations, cleaning, and feature engineering. |
| Visualization | DSC 106: Introduction to Data Visualization | 4 | Principles, techniques, and algorithms for effective interactive visualization; draws from graphics, HCI, cognitive psychology, design, and statistical graphics; includes D3 or web-based visualization systems. |
| Probabilistic AI/ML | DSC 140A **or** CSE 150A | 4 | DSC 140A covers probabilistic models for representation and decision-making, including graphical/temporal models and online learning. CSE 150A covers probabilistic reasoning and decision-making under uncertainty, Bayesian networks, Markov decision processes, and AI applications. |
| Representation learning / ML | DSC 140B **or** CSE 151A | 4 | DSC 140B covers representation learning: clustering, dimensionality reduction, PCA, spectral embeddings, neural networks, autoencoders, CNNs/RNNs, and deep learning principles. CSE 151A is a broad ML algorithms course covering supervised/unsupervised learning such as kNN, decision trees, boosting, perceptrons, k-means, and hierarchical clustering. |
| Data mining / recommender systems | DSC 148 **or** CSE 158/CSE 158R | 4 | DSC 148 covers data mining methods for real-world data: frequent patterns, regression/classification, clustering, and representation learning. CSE 158/CSE 158R covers recommender systems, web mining, predictive analytics, real datasets, working systems, and applied ML research ideas. |
| Senior project | DSC 180A-B | 8 | Two-quarter project sequence spanning problem definition, domain knowledge, data collection/cleaning, modeling, ethics, system design, analysis, presentation, and evaluation. 180A focuses on research/methodology/system design; 180B focuses on implementation and evaluation. |

## UCSD Elective Requirement

UCSD requires **20 units of upper-division electives**. These are options, not all required courses.

| Elective bucket | Eligible options | What the bucket lets you emphasize |
|---|---|---|
| Upper-division DSC electives | Any upper-division DSC course not already used for another major requirement, except DSC 197, 198, and 199. | More data science depth: advanced data systems, optimization, signal/spatial data, text as data, algorithmic fairness, topics seminars, etc., depending on offerings and prerequisites. |
| Approved domain electives | Approved courses across Biology, Cognitive Science, CSE, Economics, Environmental Systems/MAE, Linguistics, Math, Management, Political Science, Psychology, Scripps, Sociology, Urban Studies/Planning, and related cross-listed courses. | Domain depth: science, social science, business/econometrics/statistics, machine learning/AI, policy, environment, urban analysis, psychology/neuroscience, and computing. |
| Petitioned domain electives | Up to 8 units may be petitioned from upper-division courses not on the list if they fit your domain of interest. | Lets you shape a more custom applied data science domain, subject to approval and prerequisites. |

## Bottom Line

| If you value... | Advantage |
|---|---|
| Shorter, more predictable upper-division sequence after transfer | UCLA |
| Stronger traditional statistics/probability/regression backbone | UCLA |
| More data engineering, scalable analytics, visualization, and project work | UCSD |
| Broader domain-elective flexibility | UCSD |
| Fastest graduation risk profile for a transfer | Likely UCLA, assuming your lower-division prep is fully cleared |
| Most direct “data science program” branding/capstone structure | UCSD |

## Career Outcomes, Internships, and Top-Company Placement

### Important Data Limitation

Neither UCLA nor UCSD appears to publish a clean, official, major-specific statistic for **“percentage of Statistics/Data Science majors who land a top company right after graduation.”** The public data is mostly campus-wide, survey-based, or employer-list based. So the most honest comparison is:

1. what jobs each curriculum prepares you for,
2. what internship/project pipelines each campus gives you,
3. what official career outcome signals exist,
4. how transfer timing affects your odds.

In other words, a “top company success rate” is not something I would quote as a hard number for either major. I would judge it by curriculum fit, internship access, portfolio strength, recruiting ecosystem, and time-to-degree.

### Career Fit by Major

| Career path | UCLA Statistics & Data Science | UCSD Data Science |
|---|---|---|
| Data analyst / product analyst / business analyst | Very strong. Regression, experimental design, statistical inference, and R-based computation are directly useful for analytics roles. | Very strong. Data management, visualization, scalable analytics, ML, and senior project align well with applied analytics roles. |
| Data scientist, entry level | Strong if you build Python/SQL/ML projects outside required coursework. The major gives a stronger statistics base than many DS programs. | Stronger default alignment. Required database/systems/visualization/ML/project sequence maps more directly to entry-level DS job descriptions. |
| Machine learning engineer / AI engineer | Possible but not automatic. UCLA Stats & DS is not a CS-heavy major, so you would need extra Python, algorithms, software engineering, and ML projects. | Better positioned than UCLA for ML/AI-adjacent roles because of DSC/CSE options, but still not the same as a full CS major. You would still need strong coding/interview prep. |
| Analytics engineering / data engineering | Weaker by default; you would need SQL, cloud, pipelines, and software projects outside the core. | Stronger by default because DSC 100/102 cover data management and scalable analytics. |
| Quant / finance / actuarial / risk | Stronger fit because of probability, mathematical statistics, linear models, and approved math/econ/stat electives. | Possible through MATH/ECON electives, but the core is broader data science rather than stats/finance-heavy. |
| Consulting / business analytics | Strong UCLA brand plus LA network; good fit if paired with case prep, internships, and communication-heavy projects. | Good fit, especially through business/econometrics/domain electives and project work. |
| Research assistant / grad school prep | Strong for statistics, biostatistics, applied stats, data science, economics, social science methods. | Strong for data science, ML, computational social science, systems/data management, HCI, bio/data science, and applied domains. |

But you still need to recruit quickly, often starting in your first fall quarter. | Potential disadvantage: UCSD publicly warns that the DSC major can require a minimum of 8 quarters for transfers. ### Estimated Advantage by Career Goal

| Goal | Better choice | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Fastest path to employable analytics role | UCLA | Smaller upper-division requirement and strong statistics foundation, assuming prep is complete. |
| Most direct data science job curriculum | UCSD | More explicit DS systems, visualization, ML, and project sequence. |
| Big Tech data scientist / product analyst | Tie, slight UCSD curriculum edge | UCLA has brand breadth; UCSD has more directly matched technical coursework. Portfolio/internship matters more than the school difference. |
| Big Tech ML engineer / AI engineer | UCSD | More ML/CSE alignment, though CS-level preparation is still needed. |
| Quant/statistical modeling/grad stats | UCLA | Stronger probability/statistics/linear-model sequence. |

### My Career Recommendation

If your priority is **graduating on time and getting a strong analyst/data-science-adjacent job quickly**, UCLA looks safer. The Stats & DS major is compact, respected, and gives you a strong statistical spine. To compete for top tech, you would need to deliberately add Python, SQL, machine learning projects, and maybe cloud/data engineering work.

If your priority is **becoming a more technical data scientist**, UCSD’s curriculum is more purpose-built: databases, scalable analytics, visualization, ML, data mining, and a senior project. The tradeoff is serious for a transfer: if UCSD makes you take a long lower-division DSC sequence, the career upside may come with extra time and cost.

For **top-company placement right after graduation**, I would not choose based on school name alone. I would choose the place where you can realistically complete the major, secure at least one internship, and graduate with 2-3 strong projects. A UCLA student with an internship and polished portfolio will beat a UCSD student without one, and vice versa.
